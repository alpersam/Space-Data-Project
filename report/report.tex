\section{Research Question}
\label{sec:rq}

Brazil's Cerrado and Mato Grosso region is responsible for roughly
half of global soybean exports. When droughts hit this area, crop
yields fall and soybean prices on commodity markets tend to rise.
The problem is that monitoring soil moisture at the scale of an
entire agricultural region is not practically possible from the
ground. The GRACE and GRACE-FO satellite missions \cite{tapley2004grace, landerer2020extending} measure changes
in terrestrial water storage (TWS) every month at continental scale,
which gives us an independent way to track drought conditions that
could potentially predict price movements before harvest data
becomes available.

\begin{tcolorbox}[boxstyle, title=Research Question]
  \textit{To what extent do GRACE-derived terrestrial water storage
  anomalies over Brazil's central-southern agricultural belt
  (Mato Grosso, Goiás, Paraná) anticipate variations in
  international soybean futures prices, and what is the temporal
  lag structure between hydrological stress and commodity market
  response?}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Data and Methods}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Datasets}

We used five datasets in this analysis. After aligning them to the
same time axis, we ended up with 214 months of data from
April 2002 to December 2025 with no missing months.

\begin{tabularx}{\columnwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
  \toprule
  \textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{Details} \\
  \midrule
  GRACE RL06.3M v04 & JPL mascon solution, 0.5°, monthly, Apr 2002 - Dec 2025 \\
  ERA5-Land P-ET  & ECMWF reanalysis \cite{hersbach2020era5}, 0.25°, monthly, 2002-2025 \\
  MODIS MOD13A3     & Vegetation index (NDVI) from Google Earth Engine \cite{gorelick2017gee, didan2021mod13a3}, cropland pixels only \\
  Soybean ZS=F      & CME Group futures via \texttt{yfinance}, 2002-2025 \\
  ENSO ONI          & NOAA sea surface temperature index, 1950-2025 \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabularx}

\subsection{Study Region and Land Masking}

We chose the bounding box between $25^\circ$S-$8^\circ$S latitude
and $60^\circ$W-$46^\circ$W longitude because it covers the three
main soybean-producing states: Mato Grosso, Goiás, and Paraná
(Figure~\ref{fig:spatial}, top). We excluded ocean pixels using the
JPL land mask before computing the regional average, because GRACE
mascons near coastlines can pick up ocean tidal signals that have
nothing to do with land hydrology.

\subsection{Gain Factors}

The JPL mascon product \cite{watkins2015improved} requires scale factors, called gain factors,
to correct for signal loss introduced during the spherical harmonic
processing of the raw satellite data. Without applying them, the
amplitude of water storage changes is underestimated by roughly
20-40\% in areas with large variability. We applied the gain factors
from the companion JPL file to each grid cell before computing the
regional average.

\subsection{Handling the GRACE Data Gap}

The 2017-2018 gap between the GRACE and GRACE-FO missions is filled
internally in the v04 mascon product, so we did not need to
interpolate anything manually. We still excluded those months from
the Granger causality tests to be safe.

\subsection{EOF Analysis}

We used Empirical Orthogonal Function (EOF) analysis to extract
the dominant pattern of water storage variability from the full
two-dimensional GRACE grid. Rather than simply averaging all grid
cells, EOF analysis separates the main regional signal from local
noise. We used an area-weighted covariance matrix so that larger
cells near the edge of the region do not have more influence than
they should. The first EOF (EOF1) explained 58.5\% of the
total variance, which is much more than EOF2 at 19.9\%. This large
gap between the two means that one single pattern dominates, so we
used its time series (PC1) as our drought index throughout the rest
of the analysis. Using PC1 instead of the raw area-average also
reduces the effect of small-scale patterns that are not related to
agricultural drought across the whole region.

\subsection{Statistical Methods}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test \cite{dickey1979distribution}:} We tested each
        time series for stationarity before running any regression
        or correlation. Non-stationary series were first-differenced
        until they passed the test. This step is necessary to avoid
        finding spurious correlations between trending series.
  \item \textbf{Bootstrap cross-correlation:} We computed the
        Pearson correlation between the TWS index and soybean
        returns at different time lags (0 to 12 months). We used
        1000 bootstrap samples to build confidence intervals.
        A positive lag means TWS leads the price series.
  \item \textbf{Granger causality \cite{granger1969investigating}:} This test checks whether past
        values of TWS help predict soybean returns beyond what the
        price series alone can predict. We tested lags 1 through 6
        and also ran the test in the reverse direction to confirm
        the causal direction.
  \item \textbf{ENSO partial correlation:} We removed the influence
        of El Niño/La Niña (measured by the ONI index) from both
        series to check whether the TWS signal is driven by ENSO
        or carries independent information.
  \item \textbf{Sobel mediation test:} We tested whether NDVI acts
        as an intermediate link in the chain from TWS to price, or
        whether the effect is direct.
\end{itemize}

\section{Analysis Workflow}
\label{sec:workflow}

The full analysis is contained in a single notebook,
\texttt{main.ipynb}, which can be run from top to bottom. The steps
are as follows:

{\small\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=0pt, topsep=1pt, parsep=0pt]
  \item Load and mask the GRACE NetCDF file, apply the land mask and
        study region bounding box, and multiply by gain factors.
  \item Compute the area-weighted regional TWS time series using
        $\bar{h}(t) = \sum_i w_i \cdot h_i(t)$ where weights are
        proportional to $\cos\phi_i$.
  \item Run EOF decomposition on the detrended TWS grid and extract
        PC1 as the drought index.
  \item Download ERA5 precipitation minus evapotranspiration from
        the Copernicus CDS and regrid to 0.5 degrees.
  \item Pull the cropland-masked NDVI time series from Google Earth
        Engine and save it to a local CSV file.
  \item Download soybean futures from Yahoo Finance, resample to
        monthly frequency, and compute log-returns.
  \item Run ADF tests on all series and their first differences.
  \item Compute the bootstrap cross-correlation and find the best
        predictive lag.
  \item Run partial correlation after removing the ENSO signal.
  \item Run Granger causality in both directions for lags 1-6.
  \item Run the Sobel mediation test for the TWS to NDVI to price path.
  \item Produce the event study for the 2005, 2010, 2015, and 2021
        droughts.
  \item Run the illustrative backtest and export all figures.
\end{enumerate}}

\subsection{TWS Signal and EOF Decomposition}

    variance). Bottom right: PC1 time series used as the drought index;

  \label{fig:spatial}

The regional TWS anomaly ranged from $-33.14$ cm during the 2021
drought to $+26.10$ cm during a wet period, with a long-term mean
of $-3.52$ cm and a standard deviation of $13.79$ cm. The long-term
drying trend visible in Figure~\ref{fig:spatial} (top) is mirrored
by the EOF1 loading pattern (bottom left), which is concentrated over
the agricultural belt. The PC1 time series (bottom right) clearly
captures the four drought episodes we focused on.

\subsection{Multi-Signal Overview}

Figure~\ref{fig:overlay} shows all four main time series together.
Looking at the plot, TWS minima tend to occur a few months before
soybean price spikes, which is the pattern we expected based on the
growing season calendar. The ERA5 water balance and NDVI series
broadly track the TWS signal, though with less amplitude.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig3_overlay.png}
  \caption{All four time series plotted together for 2002-2025:
    GRACE TWS anomaly (blue), MODIS cropland NDVI anomaly (green),
    ERA5 precipitation minus evapotranspiration (orange), and soybean
    log-returns (red). The dashed vertical lines mark drought onset
    months and the arrows indicate the approximate five-month lead
    of TWS before the price response.}
  \label{fig:overlay}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Cross-Correlation and Optimal Lag}

The cross-correlation analysis shows the strongest negative
correlation at lag 5 months ($r = -0.277$, with a 95\% confidence
interval bound of $\pm 0.134$). The negative sign makes physical
sense: when TWS decreases (drought), soybean prices tend to increase
five months later. Lags 4 and 6 are also statistically significant
($r = -0.183$ and $r = -0.166$), suggesting the predictive signal
is spread across a window of a few months rather than concentrated
at a single lag. The bootstrap CCF plot and Granger p-value bar chart
are available in the Colab notebook.

The 60-month rolling correlation at lag 5 is negative in all windows
across the full 2002-2025 period, with a mean of $-0.291$, confirming
the relationship is not caused by a single unusual episode but holds
consistently over time (rolling correlation plot available in the
Colab notebook).

\subsection{Spectral Coherence}

The squared spectral coherence between the TWS index and soybean
log-returns peaks at 0.571 near a period of 9.6 months, consistent
with the agricultural growing season repeating on a roughly yearly
cycle (coherence plot available in the Colab notebook).

One important caveat: the phase-derived lag at the 9.6-month dominant
frequency is only about 0.5 months, which appears to contradict the
5-month predictive lag found in the cross-correlation analysis. These
two quantities are not directly comparable. The spectral phase measures
the average timing offset at the \emph{annual cycle} frequency
specifically, whereas the CCF lag of 5 months reflects the
\emph{broadband} lag structure that includes lower-frequency
inter-annual variability linked to drought events. The 5-month CCF
result should be interpreted as the predictive horizon and the
spectral analysis as a confirmation of seasonal co-variation, not as
an independent lag estimate.

\subsection{ENSO Control and Granger Causality}

When we removed the ENSO signal from both time series, the
correlation at lag 5 barely changed: partial $r = -0.275$ compared
to the raw $r = -0.277$. This means that the relationship between
TWS and soybean prices is not simply a reflection of El Niño or
La Niña events; GRACE is capturing drought information that is
unrelated to the state of the tropical Pacific.

The Granger causality test shows that past values of $\Delta$TWS
significantly improve the prediction of soybean returns at lag 5
($F = 4.27$, $p = 0.001$). The results are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:granger}. Importantly, the reverse test (whether
soybean prices help predict TWS) is not significant ($p = 0.766$),
which confirms that the causal direction runs from water storage
to prices and not the other way around.

\subsection{Causal Chain and Backtest}

Figure~\ref{fig:causal} shows the bootstrap cross-correlation at
each step of the proposed causal chain. The TWS$\to$NDVI step
(top panel) peaks at lag 7 months with $r = -0.084$, which is not
significant. The NDVI$\to$price step (middle panel) peaks at lag
3 months with $r = -0.134$, also not significant. Only the direct
TWS$\to$price path (bottom panel) is statistically significant,
peaking at lag 5 months with $r = -0.277$ ($p < 0.001$). The
Sobel mediation test confirms that NDVI carries none of the effect
(indirect path $p = 0.908$), so the dominant pathway is direct.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\columnwidth]{fig5_causal_chain.png}
  \caption{Bootstrap cross-correlation at each causal-chain step.
    Gray bands: 95\% CI; dashed verticals: best lag.}
  \label{fig:causal}
\end{figure}

The illustrative backtest tests a simple strategy: go long on soybean
futures when the TWS z-score drops below $-1\sigma$, entering five
months later. The strategy was active in only 15\% of months but
achieved a Sharpe ratio of \textbf{0.34} compared to 0.15 for a
simple buy-and-hold approach, with a much smaller maximum drawdown
of $-24\%$ versus $-63\%$. The cumulative return chart is available
in the Colab notebook.

\section{Interpretation and Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Physical Interpretation of the Five-Month Lag}

The five-month lag fits well with the Brazilian soybean growing
calendar. Droughts typically develop during the austral winter
(July to September), which is when soil moisture should be
recharging ahead of the planting season starting in October.
If water storage is already low by August, yield forecasts for
the upcoming harvest start to be revised downward in
January and February. Soybean futures prices then reflect these
expected losses roughly four to six months after the initial
drought signal, just before the main delivery period in April
and May.

\subsection{Why NDVI Does Not Act as an Intermediary}

We expected NDVI to sit between TWS and price as the visible sign
of crop stress. Neither step of the proposed chain was statistically
confirmed: the Granger test for the TWS-to-NDVI path was not
significant ($p = 0.645$), and the NDVI-to-soybean path was also
not significant ($p = 0.236$ at best lag). The Sobel mediation
test produced an indirect effect that is effectively zero
(proportion mediated $= 0.0\%$, $p = 0.908$). The three-step
causal chain TWS $\to$ NDVI $\to$ price is therefore not supported
statistically at monthly resolution; the dominant pathway appears
to be direct.

One explanation is that vegetation stress develops and recovers on
timescales of days to two weeks, which is much faster than a monthly
satellite composite can capture. It is also possible that the IGBP
cropland mask we used does not perfectly match the soybean growing
areas, which would dilute the NDVI signal. In practice, the futures
market likely reacts to higher frequency data such as weekly NDVI or
crop condition reports from agricultural agencies, rather than to
the monthly average we computed here.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Soybean futures are a global price and also reflect
        harvests in Argentina and the United States. Droughts
        elsewhere or good harvests in competing countries can
        offset the Brazilian signal. This limitation is further
        amplified by our use of CME Group futures (Chicago
        Mercantile Exchange, dollar-denominated contracts settled
        under the CME clearing system): the choice of this readily
        accessible dataset may itself heavily impact results, since
        the CME price reflects global USD-denominated contract
        flows rather than conditions specific to Brazil.
  \item The GRACE spatial resolution of around 300 km means that
        our regional average mixes different land cover types. Some
        of what we measure as TWS change may come from areas that
        are not actually growing soybeans.
  \item We only have four major drought events in our study period,
        which limits how much we can say statistically from the
        event study alone. Two of the four events (2015, 2021) did
        not produce a detectable soybean price reaction by the
        event-study algorithm, likely because competing supply
        factors dominated in those seasons.
  \item The proposed three-step causal chain (TWS $\to$ NDVI $\to$
        price) is not supported by the Granger or Sobel tests at
        monthly resolution. The analysis supports only the direct
        TWS $\to$ price link.
  \item If commodity traders start systematically using GRACE data,
        the market would likely price in the drought signal faster
        and the five-month predictive window would shrink.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{One Thing That Surprised Us}

The result that surprised us most was how little the ENSO index
mattered. Before running the analysis, we assumed that most of the
drought signal detected by GRACE in Brazil would simply be a
reflection of El Niño and La Niña events, since it is well known
that ENSO strongly influences rainfall in South America. If that
were true, GRACE would just be a slow and indirect way of measuring
something that can already be tracked through sea surface
temperatures. Instead, removing the ENSO signal from both series
changed the correlation by only $\Delta r = 0.002$. This suggests
that GRACE is picking up drought conditions that are driven by
other factors, possibly including changes in land use and
deforestation in the Cerrado that affect local precipitation
independently of ENSO. This finding makes the GRACE signal more
interesting as a standalone indicator, rather than just a proxy
for climate indices that are already well monitored.

\FloatBarrier
\section*{Conclusion}

GRACE and GRACE-FO water storage anomalies over Brazil's soybean
belt are a statistically significant predictor of CME soybean
futures prices at a five-month horizon ($r = -0.277$,
Granger $p = 0.001$). The signal is consistent over time, is not
explained by ENSO, and the causal direction from water storage to
prices is confirmed. The dominant drought mode captured by EOF1
(58.5\% of variance) provides a simple single-index summary of
regional water stress that translates satellite hydrology into
a measurable commodity market signal.
